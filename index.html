You Don't Need to Be a Designer

Design affects 80% of purchase decisions.
Here's how to diagnose it.

The user "hires" your product to do a job. Bad design makes that job harder — and leads to guaranteed losses. You don't need a designer's eye. You need a framework.

👁

Step 1 — Diagnose

Take a screenshot. Ask Claude: "What are the 3 biggest UX problems here? What psychological principles are violated?"

🔬

Step 2 — Ask users

Don't ask what they want. Ask about the last time they used it. What was hard? What did they skip? Facts, not opinions.

🧪

Step 3 — Hypothesis

"If we [change X], then [metric] will improve for [user segment]." Now test it.

❌ Weak hypothesis

"If we add a background to the push widget, CTR will grow for old users."

No segment clarity. No causal logic. Cosmetic fix.

✓ Strong hypothesis

"If we show rarely-active users their best unseen content (not latest), Smiles Rate and watch depth will grow for that cohort — and retention follows."

Psychological Principles That Actually Move Metrics

How you say it matters
as much as what you say.

🖼 Framing Effect

The same fact, framed differently, drives different behavior.

❌ "80% churn rate" same data ✓ "20% retention"
❌ "Book now" same action ✓ "Check availability"

→ Use with Claude: "Reframe this CTA to feel less committing"

😰 Loss Aversion

Fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain. But use it ethically — to help users, not manipulate.

❌ "At your own risk" shame
✓ "You could lose 17 years of Gmail history"

🔍 "Ask Mom" Test — 3 rules

Before you fix anything — talk to 3 users. But ask the right way:

✓ Ask about past facts, not future intentions
✓ Ask about their life, not your idea
✓ Look for stories and specifics, not opinions

"What was the hardest part last time you ordered?" >> "Would you use this feature?"

How you say it matters as much as what you say

7 things to check.
A Claude prompt for each.

📋
1. Clear value proposition
State exactly what the user gets in exchange for their action.
"Rewrite this headline so a 5th grader instantly knows what they get and why it matters now."
👥
2. Social proof
Show how many people already use / trust your product.
"Suggest 3 ways to add social proof to this screen using the numbers I have: [X users, Y reviews]."
🤝
3. Respectful UX & copy
No shame. Always provide an exit. Respect the user's choice.
"Does any text on this screen shame or pressure the user? Rewrite to be respectful while keeping conversion intent."
⚡
4. Instant feedback
Deliver value immediately after user takes action.
"What confirmation or reward should the user see within 2 seconds of completing this action? Give 3 options."
🚫
5. Remove distractions
One screen, one goal. Kill everything that competes for attention.
"List every element on this screen competing with the main CTA. Which 3 can I remove or hide without losing value?"
🪜
6. Break into steps
Complex forms = paralysis. Split into small, manageable steps.
"Split this [form/flow] into steps. Use Zeigarnik effect — show progress and what's already done to keep users going."
💎
7. Remind of value at every step
Reinforce what the user will get. Especially when the task is long or hard — show how far they've come.
"At each step of this [checkout/onboarding/form], add a line that reminds the user what they'll get at the end. Keep it under 8 words."
The Hard Part — Unit Economics

AI output ≠ business value

The Surprise

Our AI recommendations were technically perfect — high conversion on popular products.

Zero margin.

AI optimized for engagement. We needed it to optimize for contribution margin.

If you can't calculate the cost of one AI response, you're not ready for production.

3 Questions Before Any AI Feature

1

Which business metric are we moving?

Not clicks. Not engagement. A line on the P&L. Define it before writing the prompt.

2

What does one AI response cost?

Tokens + human review time + prompt maintenance. Hidden costs kill unit economics.

3

Where is "good enough"?

AI doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be profitable. Set the threshold before launch.

Framework — Take This With You

3 rules of data-driven prototyping

01

Data in the prototype — from day one

Not "we'll add real content later." Real data is not a polish step. It's the design tool. A prototype without it is a hallucination, not a validation.

02

AI optimizes what you give it

Give it clicks → it optimizes clicks. Give it margin → it optimizes margin. The metric you choose in the prompt is the metric you'll get in production.

03

Speed of validation > beauty of prototype

An ugly prototype with real data beats a beautiful mockup with lorem ipsum. Always. The goal is to be wrong fast, not to look right slowly.
